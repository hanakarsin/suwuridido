Kenya among African countries to struggle repaying debt - World Bank

Publish date: 2024-03-29

- The bank said sub-Saharan Africa's overall economic growth will drop from initially projected 3.1% to 2.7%

- The snail-paced economic growth was attributed to sluggish expansion in the region's biggest economies

- In Kenya, the economy is steady, but the World Bank warned all may not be well for the country moving forward

- Rising public debt, weakening shilling and soaring interests are likely to affect Kenya's ability to service loans

- Kenya's public debt staggered at KSh 5.1 trillion as at September 2018 and is expected to rise in the next four years

The World Bank on Wednesday, October 3, revised downwards its economic growth forecast for sub-Saharan Africa from its initial prediction of 3.1% in April 2018 to 2.7%, TUKO.co.ke has learnt.

The bank attributed the drop in the average growth rate to sluggish recovery in the region's top three biggest economies; South Africa, Nigeria and Angola, which lost growth impetus following the commodity prices crash that hit Africa in 2015.

The bank noted economic growth in the rest of the African countries, most of which do not depend on commodities like oil and gold, including Kenya, remained relatively steady in 2018.

However, the global lender's chief economist for Africa, Albert Zeufack, warned wastage of public funds, rising interest rates, and weakening currencies were likely to affect the countries' abilities to repay their debts.

“Policymakers in the region must equip themselves to manage new risks arising from changes in the composition of capital flows and debt,” Zeufack said.

In Kenya, external and internal debts, collectively referred to as national, public or government debt, hit KSh 5.1 trillion in September 2018 and is expected to increase by KSh 2 trillion in the next four years.

A draft Budget Review and Outlook Paper to be submitted to Parliament projected the debt to rise by an additional KSh 2 trillion in the next four years.

Going by the exchequer's projections, the total public debt would grow from the estimated KSh 5 trillion in June 2018 to about KSh 7.17 trillion in June 2022.

TUKO.co.ke understands a huge percentage of the revenue generated through taxes has been going towards servicing the existing loans, thus leaving less money for development and recurrent expenditures.

With a huge chunk of revenue repaying loans, finding money to finance the national budget has increasingly become a challenge.

This partly explains why the Treasury came up with a raft of austerity measures and new taxes in attempts to reduce the budget deficit and raise funds for Uhuru's Big Four agenda.
